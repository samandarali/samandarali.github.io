Labour Market Landscape for Economics Graduates in Canada

A comprehensive data-driven investigation using web-scraping and natural language processing to analyze demands and skill requirements for economics graduates in the Canadian job market.

Research Overview

Using web-scraping and natural language processing techniques, this study investigates the demands and skill requirements for economics graduates in the Canadian job market. Key regional hubs identified are Ontario (Toronto), Quebec (Montreal), and Alberta (Calgary).

Microsoft Office Suite, particularly Microsoft Excel, is the most prevalent software requirement. Beyond software, economics graduates are expected to have a wide range of technical skills, including data management, advanced modelling, programming (Python, SQL), and presentation and documentation (PowerPoint, report writing).

Research Gap

While web-scraping research has been applied to computer science fields, there was a lack of similar research specifically focused on economics graduates in Canada.

Data Source

Data exclusively sourced from Indeed.ca on January 15, 2024, providing a real-time snapshot of the Canadian economics job market.

Research Methodology

1

Data Collection

Scraped 996 job listings from Indeed.ca using a custom Python script executed on Google Cloud Notebook. The script used BeautifulSoup for web scraping and integrated the Scrapingdog API to extract job titles, company names, locations, and hyperlinks.

Date: January 15, 2024
Pages scraped: 69 most recent pages
2

Data Cleaning & Translation

Eliminated duplicates, resulting in 606 unique job listings. Translated 134 French and 5 Spanish listings using OpenAI, with manual error correction. Removed 81 listings (75 requiring other degrees, 6 academic positions), focusing on 525 non-academic jobs.

3

Keyword Extraction & Skill Mapping

Used NLP techniques (tokenization, lemmatization, stop-word removal) to extract skills. Applied OpenAI GPT-3.5-turbo API to categorize soft and technical skills. Created comprehensive dictionaries: 31 soft skill categories and 30 technical skill categories.

Key Innovation: The comprehensive skill dictionaries created provide a transparent, accessible resource for future labor market studies.

4

Network Analysis & Statistical Testing

Applied centrality metrics (degree, betweenness, closeness) to evaluate skill significance. Used Chi-square and Fisher's exact tests to identify regional variations in skill demands across provinces.

Key Insights

High-Importance Skills: Data Management and Presentation & Documentation emerge as the most frequently required technical skills, underscoring their foundational role across diverse job postings. Financial Analysis & Management is another critical skill but is less common compared to the above.

Frequent Pairings: The pairing of Data Management with Presentation & Documentation suggests that employers value candidates who can not only handle data but also effectively communicate insights through reports and presentations. Similarly, the combination of Financial Analysis & Management with Data Management indicates the reliance on data for financial decision-making and reporting.

Comprehensive Skillsets: The three-skill combination (Financial Analysis & Management, Data Management, Presentation & Documentation) reflects demand for highly versatile professionals who can bridge technical and business communication needs.

Emerging Skills: Advanced Modeling & Analytics and Econometrics & Statistics have relatively lower support but remain critical in specialized roles, particularly those requiring advanced analytical techniques.

These findings not only reinforce the importance of technical skills for economics graduates but also highlight how employers value the interplay between data, analysis, and communication. The alignment of results between association analysis and clustering further strengthens the reliability of these insights.
