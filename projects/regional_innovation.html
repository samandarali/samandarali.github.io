A regional innovation study using a spatial knowledge production function framework with Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and spatial spillover testing, built on a proprietary domestic patent dataset.

Research Overview

This project explains why some regions are more innovative than others by modeling regional patenting intensity (patents per capita) using a knowledge production function framework and PCA-based dimension reduction. It analyzes the landscape of regional innovation and patenting activity in Iran between 2008 and 2012. Using a spatial knowledge production function, the research identifies that individual inventors dominate the system, while company-led patenting remains unusually low compared to global trends. The data highlights a geographic concentration of innovation around Tehran and reveals a significant lack of knowledge spillovers between neighboring regions.

Research questions

  • Which factors explain regional innovative output the most?
  • Do drivers differ by institutional ownership (individuals vs. universities vs. companies)?
  • Is there evidence of spatial spillovers between neighboring regions?

Why the data matters

The study is enabled by a proprietary dataset of domestic granted patents, built directly from official sources, which makes regional quantitative analysis possible where it previously wasn’t.

Impact & Beneficiaries

The research presented in the sources is primarily beneficial for policymakers, the academic community, and industrial stakeholders within Iran and similar developing economies. By identifying the specific factors that drive innovation in a unique context—where economic sanctions are prevalent and individual inventors outnumber corporate ones—the study provides a roadmap for fostering economic growth.

🏛️

National and Regional Policymakers

  • Strategic Planning: Policymakers can use the research to understand why innovation is geographically centralized and why knowledge fails to "spill over" into neighboring regions.
  • Commercialization: The study highlights an "urgent need" for policies that encourage individual inventors to collaborate with and commercialize their ideas through Iranian companies.
  • Promoting Competition: The research suggests that promoting market competition is a viable way to boost regional innovative output, as monopoly power was found to have a negative effect.

The Academic and Scientific Community

  • New Methodologies: It demonstrates how the Griliches–Jaffe knowledge production function and Principal Component Analysis (PCA) can be applied to study innovation in developing countries.
  • Foundation for Future Study: It provides a baseline for future researchers to investigate smaller geographical scales (like cities) or longer time spans to better understand regional dynamics.
🏭

Individual Inventors and Private Companies

  • For Companies: It identifies that R&D investment and human capital (lecturers and students) are the most influential factors for increasing company-owned patents.
  • For Individual Inventors: It recognizes them as the "main players" of the Iranian patent system (owning 87% of patents) and explores how welfare and standard of living directly impact their ability to innovate.
🗺️

Regional Developers

Since technological innovation is a "major driving force" behind economic growth and prosperity, regional planners can use this data to identify which "contextual" or "industrial" indexes are lacking in their specific provinces to help boost local development.

Dataset Novelty & Construction

The primary tool for data collection was a proprietary dataset built by manually recording patent information from the "Iranian Official Journal" on a daily basis. Additional regional data were pulled from the Statistical Centre of Iran (SCI) and the Institute for Research and Planning in Higher Education (IRPHE).

Why this dataset is novel

  • First-of-its-kind domestic collection: built from domestic granted patent records rather than international databases.
  • Manual construction from official records: recorded directly from daily official publications when no open public database existed.
  • Researcher-assigned classifications: required assigning IPC codes because they were not provided in the official advertisements.
  • Rich bibliographic scope: includes inventor/applicant information and enables regional clustering by inventor address.

What the article uses

The article analyzes a subsample covering 18,717 resident-granted patents during 2008–2010, clustered across 30 regions. Patents are assigned to provinces based on the address of the first inventor and categorized by institutional ownership.

Methodology

1

Theoretical Framework — Knowledge Production Function

The study applies the Griliches–Jaffe Knowledge Production Function (KPF), a geographically extended version of the original innovation production model. It assumes that regional innovative output depends on both internal inputs and external regional characteristics.

The model is specified in a logarithmic Cobb–Douglas form, allowing the elasticities of innovation output with respect to R&D, human capital, and structural factors to be estimated.

Innovation output is proxied by patents per capita, estimated for total patents and by ownership category (personal, university, company).

2

Dimensionality Reduction — Principal Component Analysis

Fourteen highly correlated regional indicators created multicollinearity and dimensionality challenges. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was used to transform them into a smaller set of orthogonal components.

Contextual Index
R&D + human capital + diffusion capacity
Industrial Index
Industrial structure & specialization
Low-Welfare Index
Employment, literacy, living conditions

Validation tests: Kaiser–Meyer–Olkin (KMO) and Bartlett’s test of sphericity confirmed the dataset’s suitability for PCA.

3

Econometric Modeling — Panel Data Analysis

The dataset covers 30 regions over three years (2008–2010), producing a balanced panel of 90 observations.

  • Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) used as a baseline estimator.
  • Random Effects (RE) models applied to account for regional heterogeneity.
  • The Hausman test confirmed RE as more efficient than Fixed Effects for this dataset.
4

Spatial Econometric Analysis

To test whether innovation diffuses geographically, the study integrates spatial dependence into the KPF framework.

  • Spatial weight matrices: constructed using inverse road distance and road travel time between regions.
  • Spatial Durbin Model: tests whether explanatory variables in one region affect neighboring innovation output.
  • Diagnostic testing: Moran’s I and Lagrange Multiplier (LM) tests identify spatial lags and spatial error structures.

These tools determine whether regional innovation is spatially interdependent or geographically contained.

Key Findings

Key Findings (Article)

Dominance of individual inventors

Most patents are owned by individuals (about 87–88%), unlike many developed economies where firms dominate. The analysis suggests individuals are not simply filing on behalf of companies, because the drivers of personal patents differ from those of company patents.

No spatial knowledge spillover

The spatial models do not support inter-regional knowledge spillovers in the study period. Innovative activity is concentrated in a small number of hubs, and knowledge externalities appear constrained by regional borders.

Low-Welfare index drives personal patents

Among the PCA-based indices, the Low-Welfare index shows the strongest relative importance for total and personal patents, linking regional standard-of-living factors (e.g., employment, literacy) to individual inventive activity.

Contextual index drives company patents

For company patents, the main driver is the Contextual index, which captures factors such as R&D investment and human capital (e.g., students and lecturers). The Industrial index has a smaller effect relative to the other two.
