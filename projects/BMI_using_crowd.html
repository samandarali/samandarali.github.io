Using the Crowd for Business Model Innovation: The Case of Digikala

Exploratory industry-collaborative case study examining how crowdsourcing can enable Business Model Innovation, based on the analysis of 1,799 proposals submitted to Digikala’s nationwide “A Glance at Tomorrow” innovation contest.

Research Overview

This groundbreaking study explores whether and how companies can leverage crowdsourcing for Business Model Innovation (BMI). While crowdsourcing has proven effective for product and process innovation, its application to BMI remains largely unexplored.

On November 15, 2015, Digikala—one of the largest online retailers in the Middle East—launched "A Glance at Tomorrow," a crowdsourcing contest inviting the public to envision the company's business model in 2021. This initiative generated 1,799 proposals, providing a unique opportunity to investigate crowd-powered BMI.

Research Context

Digikala dominated Iran's online retail market with an 85-90% market share and was valued at $150 million in 2014 by The Economist. The company's rapid growth (averaging 100% annually) and cutting-edge digital innovation made it an ideal case for studying crowd-powered BMI.

Why This Research Matters

🎯 Addressing a Research Gap

Previous crowdsourcing research focused almost entirely on product and process innovation. This study explores the uncharted territory of crowd-powered business model innovation.

💡 Practical Implications

Companies increasingly turn to BMI to remain competitive. Understanding how to leverage external crowds can provide valuable strategic insights.

📊 Real Large-Scale Data

Unlike theoretical studies, this research analyzes actual proposals from a real crowdsourcing contest, providing empirical evidence.

🔍 Division of Innovation Labor

The study reveals a new pattern in BMI where the crowd excels at certain components while firms complement with internal expertise.

Research Questions

Q1: Can the crowd contribute to Business Model Innovation?

The study investigates whether external crowds can generate valuable ideas for innovating a company's business model.

Q2: How can companies best use crowd-powered solutions?

The research examines which aspects of a business model the crowd can effectively address and how firms should integrate these insights.

Q3: What characteristics affect proposal quality?

The study analyzes how participant demographics, motivations, and experience influence the quality of BMI proposals.

Research Methodology

Case Study Approach

This exploratory case study employed multiple data collection and analysis techniques to understand crowd-powered BMI comprehensively.

The Contest: "A Glance at Tomorrow"

  • Duration: 24 days (November 15, 2015 onwards)
  • Participants: 1,799 submissions from Iranian online community
  • Challenge: Envision Digikala's business model in 2021
  • Prizes: Three grand prizes, plus additional awards for innovative proposals
  • Evaluation: Internal committee from innovation, business development, marketing, and content teams

Data Collection

Data collection consisted of two primary components:

  • Crowdsourcing Contest Analysis: The primary data source was 1,799 proposals submitted to Digikala’s "A Glance at Tomorrow" contest, which lasted 24 days.
  • Follow-up Survey: An online questionnaire was sent to participants to capture demographics, motivations, and brand engagement.

📄 Proposal Analysis

Full access to all 1,799 proposals submitted to the contest.

  • • Independent review by two researchers
  • • Cross-checking for validity
  • • Extraction of innovative ideas
  • • One-sentence summarization

📊 Survey Data

Online questionnaire sent to all participants.

  • • 844 responses (47% response rate)
  • • 794 matched with proposals
  • • Demographics and motivations
  • • Shopping experience with Digikala

Analytical Framework

Business Model Canvas Mapping

Innovative ideas were mapped onto the nine components of Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas, grouped into four building blocks:

  • Value Proposition: What value is offered to customers
  • Value Delivery: How the value reaches customers (channels, relationships)
  • Value Creation: Key activities, resources, and partnerships
  • Value Capture: Revenue streams and cost structure

Quality Grading

Digikala's internal committee categorized proposals into four quality grades:

  • Grade A: Top 3 most innovative proposals
  • Grade B: Next 10 innovative proposals
  • Grade C: 128 sufficiently innovative proposals
  • Grade D: Non-innovative or non-compliant proposals

⚠️ Methodological Rigor

To ensure validity, the research team conducted sample selection checks using T-tests and F-tests to verify that survey respondents were representative of all contest participants. The results confirmed no significant bias in the response patterns.

Key Findings

1. Division of Innovation Labor in BMI

The crowd excels at customer-facing components of business models but struggles with internal operational aspects.

This finding suggests a natural "division of labor" where external crowds contribute ideas for value proposition and delivery, while firms develop complementary solutions for value creation and capture using their internal expertise.

✅ Crowd Strengths

  • Value Proposition (1,097 ideas): What customers need
  • Value Delivery (961 ideas): How to reach customers
  • Combined: 86% of all innovative ideas

⚠️ Limited Contribution

  • Value Creation (295 ideas): Internal operations
  • Value Capture (39 ideas): Revenue and costs
  • Combined: Only 14% of innovative ideas

💡 Key Insight

All three Grade A proposals (top tier) included ideas for Value Proposition, Value Delivery, and Value Creation—but none addressed Value Capture. This pattern held for Grade B proposals as well, with only 1 out of 10 touching on revenue models.

2. Participant Characteristics Matter

👥 Demographics

  • Age: Participants aged 21-40 had significantly higher quality proposals
  • Education: Graduate degree holders produced notably better ideas
  • Gender: When controlling for proposal characteristics, female participants submitted higher quality proposals
  • Academic Major: Those with degrees in business, engineering, or ICT/computer science had an advantage over distant fields

🎯 Motivation Matters Most

The research revealed striking differences in proposal quality based on participant motivations:

Positive Motivators:

  • ✅ To have fun (62% of participants)
  • ✅ Recognition as creative (63%)
  • ✅ Both significantly increased quality

Negative Impact:

  • ❌ Prize motivation (38%)
  • ❌ Significantly lowered quality
  • ❌ Intrinsic > extrinsic motivation

🛍️ Customer Experience

Prior shopping experience with Digikala had a significant impact:

  • No shopping history: Significantly lower probability of innovative proposals
  • Any shopping experience: No significant difference between 1-5, 6-10, or 10+ purchases
  • Implication: Customer familiarity with the business model helps generate relevant ideas

📝 Proposal Characteristics

  • Length matters: More pages correlated with higher quality (effort proxy)
  • Number of ideas: More ideas per proposal increased quality probability
  • Diversity: Greater idea diversity (measured by Herfindahl Index) significantly improved quality

3. Implications for Practice

🎯

Target the Right Crowd

Focus on participants with relevant education, intrinsic motivation, and customer experience for higher quality BMI ideas.

🔄

Complement, Don't Replace

Use crowd input for customer-facing ideas while developing internal solutions for operations and revenue models.

💎

Emphasize Intrinsic Rewards

Design contests that appeal to fun and creativity rather than large monetary prizes to attract better ideas.

Comparison with Previous Research

📚 Waldner & Poetz (2015)

Previous research using BM Canvas as a template:

  • • Crowd provided ideas across ALL building blocks
  • • Including Value Capture
  • • Canvas was shown to participants
  • Key difference: Structured input

🔬 This Study (Digikala)

Open-ended crowdsourcing without templates:

  • • Natural distribution: 86% customer-facing
  • • Minimal Value Capture ideas
  • • No canvas template provided
  • Key difference: Organic patterns

💡 Critical Insight

The stark difference in Value Capture contributions between studies suggests that:

  • 1. Conflict of Interest: Crowds may avoid suggesting how to maximize company profits at their expense
  • 2. Information Asymmetry: External participants lack deep knowledge of company operations and finances
  • 3. Natural Expertise: Customers inherently understand needs and preferences better than internal workings
  • 4. Complementarity Required: Firms must develop Value Capture mechanisms internally

The New Division of Innovation Labor

🌍 External Crowd (Ideation Phase)

Strengths:

  • ✓ Customer needs understanding
  • ✓ Unmet demand identification
  • ✓ Channel and relationship ideas
  • ✓ Not burdened by dominant logic

Focus:

  • • Value Proposition innovation
  • • Value Delivery mechanisms

🏢 Internal Firm (Core BMI Process)

Strengths:

  • ✓ Deep business knowledge
  • ✓ Financial expertise
  • ✓ Operational capabilities
  • ✓ Trial-and-error learning capacity

Focus:

  • • Value Creation mechanisms
  • • Value Capture models

Key Theoretical Contribution

This study introduces the concept of "division of innovation labor" in Business Model Innovation, where external crowds provide customer-centric ideation while firms contribute operational and financial expertise—mirroring Chesbrough's (2006) division of labor concept but applied to BMI rather than product development.
